# sustained_infants_attention_bma

According to the learning progress hypothesis, infants’ attention is guided by seeking information to learn from: Infants are more likely to look away when little information is to be gained from a stimulus and continue to attend if information gain is high (Poli et al., 2020). Yet, the role of social interaction in combination with information gain remains unclear. That is particularly surprising given that a separate line of research has shown social interactions to play an important role in infants’ attention (Yu & Smith, 2016). Amongst others, previous findings suggest that infants sustain their attention for longer when being engaged in a social context (Wass et al., 2020; Yu & Smith, 2016). 

To better understand what shapes infants' attention, we investigated the role of the caregiver in conjunction with the role of information gain as potential drivers of infants’ sustained visual attention. For this purpose, we combined testing 9-month-old infants and their caregivers in a behavioral experiment with a computational modeling approach. This study was pre-registered (https://osf.io/gd6cb).

We used a computational model to analyze infants’ behavior, specifically, predicting the conditional probability of them looking away from the setup. For a more comprehensive analysis, we performed Bayesian Model Averaging. We found that information gain, the social interaction condition and the time that passed in the experiment influenced the conditional probability of infants’ lookaway, including the two-way interactions between them.
